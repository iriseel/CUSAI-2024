This course introduces students to graphic design as an expressive and communicative medium. Students are introduced to graphic design history, imagery, typography, and symbolism for visual communication. Drawing, digital photography, and Adobe software (Illustrator, Photoshop, and InDesign) are used to execute class assignments. For all assignments, visual problem solving will be emphasized. Students will also participate in demos, group discussions, guest lectures, and class critiques. This class is for young artists/designers who are excited to learn how images and type can work together to communicate ideas.

Class sessions will be structured around group critiques and informal presentations, with occasional guest critics.

Week 1: July 8-10
3 Poster Series
3 posters (24” x 36”) using images, text, and images+text about something you are interested in. Think about sequencing, cohesion, design systems, scale, and composition. In each of your posters, I recommend trying to prioritize just 1 or 2 core visual ideas inspired by your subject.
This project is divided into 3 sub-projects: 1 poster that is all image, 1 poster that is all text, and 1 poster that is both image and text. At least one of your posters must be black and white.
Monday / July 8
Introductions and course overview.
Distribute sketchbooks, pencils, erasers, rulers.
Introduction to Project: 3 Poster Series
Presentation of References (posters)
In class: Brainstorm subjects you are interested in, come up with a list of 10.
Narrow down to 2-3 to share with the class, open discussion about everyone’s subjects.
Narrow down to 1 subject. Start poster series project about your chosen subject: Begin research, both informational and visual, and start to collect images and text you would like to use. Start to develop your position — what do you want to say?
End of class: Introduction to Type in the World, a mini-project to take place over the course of the entire program
While you’re out and about, take note of interesting, attractive, ugly, or otherwise notable typography in your environment. Take pictures and upload them to our class Are.na channel. We’ll periodically do a “show-and-tell” of our selections at the start of class.

Week 3: July 22-24
Custom Typeface Design
Make a custom typeface made out of a material not traditionally used for mark-making (e.g. paper clips, rubber bands, flowers, chewed gum, fruit peels, dried pasta – anything goes!)
Use https://www.calligraphr.com/en/ to generate into usable font file.
End goal: Make a 24”x36” type specimen poster using the typeface.
